Albertosaurus
200 University Avenue West
CREATURE BIO

Introduction: The Albertosaurus, meaning “Alberta Lizard” was first discovered in 1884 by a young geologist named Joseph Burr Tyrrell who was looking for coal in Horseshoe Canyon in the Red Deer River Valley of Alberta, Canada (Royal Tyrrell Museum, 2018). Henry Fairfield Osborne, the then director of the American Museum of Natural History in New York gave the dinosaur its name in 1905 (Murray, 2018). Albertosaurus was a part of the Tyrannosaurid theropod dinosaur family (Murray, 2018).

"Even the former governor of California, could never have won an arm-wrestling competition with an Albertosaurus" (Persons, 2016).

Quote by the University of Alberta paleontologist Scott Persons who explained that the relatively small arms of the Albertosaurus were still extremely strong by human standards (referencing Arnold Schwarzenegger) (Snowden, 2016).

When & Where: Albertosaurus lived in Western Canada 70-74 million years ago during the late Cretaceous Period (Mancini, 2014). That was a period when the supercontinent, Pangaea was finishing its process of drifting apart and the land was humid, heavily vegetated and tropical to subtropical (Pika, 2018). Other dinosaurs that shared the same habitat with the Albertosaurus included Ceratopsians, Hadrosaurs, and Ornithomimids (Murray, 2018).

Evolution History: The earliest tyrannosauroids evolved near modern-day China during the Jurassic period and then spread out across Asia, Europe and the Americas (Mancini, 2014). They measured around 1.8 meters long when fully grown, which was nowhere the size of their Albertosaurus successors (Mancini, 2014).

Morphology: An adult Albertosaurus measured about 9 meters from head to tail and weighed about 2500 kilograms (Russell, 1970). The Albertosaurus was a bipedal theropod dinosaur with two powerful legs and a large head, which was about 1 meter in length (Murray, 2018). They had two short arms with two-fingered hands and a long tail, which was used to change running directions and balance their bodies (Murray, 2018). Albertosaurus had a jaw that contained over 58 banana-shaped teeth (Holtz, 2004). Compared to the Tyrannosaurus rex, Albertosaurus had a shorter, lower jaw, had shorter hip bones, and their hind lower limb bones were proportionately longer (Murray, 2018).

Studies have shown that Albertosaurus has a bite force of 42000 Newtons, which is more than two times that of a modern-day saltwater crocodile’s bite force(Jovanelly et al., 2012). There were distinctive short, bony crests, which was a unique feature above the Albertosaurus’ eyes. The crests might have been brightly coloured, which could be used to attract mates (Murray, 2018). When they were chasing prey, they were able to run 30 km/hour (Murray, 2018). It usually took them 20 years to reach maturity and their life expectancy was around 30 years (Strauss, 2018).

Diet: The Albertosaurus was a carnivorous apex predator during the upper Cretaceous Period (Royal Tyrrell Museum, 2018). Albertosaurus preyed on herbivorous dinosaurs such as Hadrosaurs, Ceratopsians, and Ornithomimids (Strauss, 2018). Fossil evidence may have shown that Albertosaurus hunted cooperatively in packs because over 20 individual Albertosaurus fossils have been found at one site (Murray, 2018). Using cooperation, they might have been able to hunt and kill bigger prey such as Sauropods (Murray, 2018).

Extinction: Albertosaurus was believed to be wiped out alongside other dinosaur species when an asteroid hit the present day Yucatan peninsula, which created the Chicxulub crater (Sumner, 2017). Such an impact would have buried plants and creatures under a pile of rubble. Debris would have darkened the sky and caused the planet to cool down for years, which killed vegetation, causing an ecosystem collapse (Sumner, 2017). In addition, bouts of volcanism that spewed molten rock and toxic gases would have also contributed to the dinosaur’s demise around 66 million years ago (Sumner, 2017).

Fun Facts:

  • Albertosaurus had a famous cousin, the Tyrannosaurus Rex. Both of them were from the Tyrannosauridae family but the Tyrannosaurus rex was much bigger than the Albertosaurus with a length of over 12 meters and 7 to 8 tons in weight (Strauss, 2018). However, the Albertosaurus was almost certainly faster than the Tyrannosaurus Rex (Strauss, 2018).

  • There were other dinosaurs that were named after Alberta such as Albertaceratops, Albertadromeus, and Albertonykus (Strauss, 2018).

  • Finding fossils of juvenile Albertosaurus are extremely rare because their less than solid bones did not preserve well as fossils (Strauss, 2018).

  • Albertosaurus grew remarkably fast in their adolescent years as they packed 230 kg per year until their maximum size (Strauss, 2018).
